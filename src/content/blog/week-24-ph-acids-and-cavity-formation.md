In chemistry this week, we did a lab measuring the pH of common household substances. We tested vinegar, baking soda, lemon juice, milk, and soap — watching the indicator change color at each step. It was simple, but it changed how I think about something I write about every week: cavities.

## Your Mouth Has a pH

Healthy saliva sits around **pH 7**, neutral. But every time you eat something sugary or starchy, bacteria in plaque feed on those carbohydrates and produce acid as a byproduct. That acid drops the pH in your mouth to around **5.5 or lower** — and that's where the trouble starts.

Tooth enamel begins to demineralize when pH drops below about 5.5. This number is called the **critical pH**, and I wrote about the damage it causes in my posts on enamel and cavities. But seeing it in a lab with actual pH paper made it click differently. It's not abstract — it's a measurable chemical shift happening in your mouth after every snack.

## Acid Attacks Are Constant

What surprised me most was thinking about the *frequency* of acid attacks. It's not about how much sugar you eat in one sitting — it's about how often you expose your teeth to acidic conditions. Each time you sip a soda or eat a piece of candy, your mouth's pH drops and stays low for 20–30 minutes before saliva neutralizes it.

In chemistry terms, it's a repeated equilibrium shift. The enamel crystal (Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂) is in equilibrium with calcium and phosphate ions dissolved in saliva. Acid pushes that equilibrium toward dissolution — pulling minerals out of the tooth.

## Buffers and Protection

We also learned about **buffers** — substances that resist pH change. Saliva acts as a natural buffer in the mouth, which is why staying hydrated matters so much for oral health. Fluoride, calcium in dairy products, and even chewing sugar-free gum (which stimulates saliva) all help restore pH balance faster.

## Connecting the Dots

This week tied together three things I've been studying separately: the pH lab in chemistry, the cavity progression I wrote about in Week 15, and the daily habits from Week 22. They're all the same story told from different angles.

The mouth isn't separate from chemistry — it *is* chemistry. Every cavity is a chemical reaction. Every brushing session is an intervention in that reaction.

## Final Thoughts

I used to think of cavities as a hygiene problem. Now I see them as a chemistry problem that hygiene prevents. That shift in perspective is exactly why I'm glad I'm taking chemistry alongside this dental journal — the two subjects are teaching each other.
