This book completely changed how I think about medical research. Henrietta Lacks was a woman whose cells were taken without her knowledge in 1951, and those cells — known as HeLa cells — became one of the most important tools in medicine.

## What I loved

Skloot weaves together science, history, and the Lacks family's personal story in a way that makes complex topics accessible. It's not just a biology book — it's about ethics, race, consent, and what we owe the people behind scientific breakthroughs.

## Key takeaway

Science doesn't happen in a vacuum. Real people are affected by research, and their stories deserve to be told. As someone interested in healthcare, this book reminded me that empathy and ethics are just as important as technical knowledge.

**Rating: 5/5** — Essential reading for anyone interested in science or medicine.
