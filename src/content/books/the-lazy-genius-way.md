As a freshman juggling chemistry, orchestra, softball, a weekly dental journal, and Care Collective, I often feel like I'm supposed to have everything figured out. *The Lazy Genius Way* didn't give me a new to-do list — it gave me permission to stop pretending I need one for everything.

## The core idea

Kendra Adachi's whole philosophy comes down to one sentence: **be a genius about what matters, and lazy about what doesn't.** That doesn't mean doing less or caring less. It means deciding where your energy actually belongs — and not wasting it on things that don't move your life forward.

For me, what matters is school, my dental journal, community work, and the people I care about. What doesn't need to be perfect? A spotless room before every study session, or saying yes to every opportunity that sounds impressive on paper.

## Principles that stuck with me

Adachi lays out thirteen Lazy Genius principles, but a few hit especially hard as a busy student:

**Decide once.** Instead of re-deciding every morning whether to floss, study chemistry first, or write my blog post, I pick a default and stop negotiating with myself. It's the same idea behind the daily habits I wrote about in Week 22 — consistency beats motivation.

**Start small.** I applied this to my dental journal itself. Week 1 wasn't a masterpiece; it was one honest post. The Lazy Genius Way validated that approach for everything else I take on.

**Ask the Magic Question:** *What can I do now to make this easier later?* I used this when planning Care Collective drives — setting up a supply list once instead of scrambling before every event.

## Why it resonated

What I appreciate most is that Adachi doesn't preach a single system. She's not telling you to wake up at 5 a.m. or color-code your entire life. She's asking you to figure out what *you* care about and build around that.

As someone interested in dentistry — a field that demands precision *and* long hours — that balance feels important. I won't survive high school, college, or dental school by doing everything at full intensity. I need to know what's worth my best effort and what's fine at "good enough."

## Final thoughts

This book isn't about shortcuts. It's about intention. It helped me think more clearly about how I spend my time outside the classroom — and feel less guilty about the things I choose not to optimize.

**Rating: 5/5** — Especially useful if you're busy, ambitious, and tired of feeling like you're falling short of someone else's definition of success.
